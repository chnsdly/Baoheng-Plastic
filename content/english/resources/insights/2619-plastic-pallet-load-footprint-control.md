A plastic pallet can be correctly rated and still perform poorly if the load footprint is wrong.

In real warehouses, many pallet failures do not begin with a single overload event. They begin with cartons hanging past the pallet edge, drums sitting on narrow contact rings, bags sagging into deck openings, or mixed SKUs creating uneven pressure on one corner. The pallet may not break immediately, but the operation starts to see cracked deck ribs, unstable stacks, damaged packaging, forklift rework, and disputes over whether the problem is product, pallet, or handling.

For B2B buyers and warehouse managers, the practical question is simple:

**How should product footprint and load contact be controlled before choosing a plastic pallet for daily operations?**

This guide focuses on that decision.

---

## 1) Start with the contact pattern, not only the total weight

Load capacity numbers are useful, but they are not enough. A 1,000 kg load spread evenly across cartons behaves very differently from a 1,000 kg load concentrated through four small feet.

Before approving a pallet, document how the product actually touches the deck:

- full-bottom cartons or cases,
- sacks or bags that deform under pressure,
- drums, pails, or buckets with circular bases,
- crates with feet or ribs,
- machinery parts with narrow supports,
- mixed SKU layers with different carton sizes.

The goal is to understand whether the pallet receives a distributed load or a concentrated point load. Plastic pallets are engineered with ribs, legs, runners, and deck openings. When the product footprint aligns with those support zones, performance is predictable. When the load sits between support zones, deck deflection and local stress can rise quickly.

A practical RFQ should therefore ask for more than static and dynamic load ratings. It should include a sketch or photo of the product base, palletizing pattern, and expected stack height. If your team already uses a sourcing template, add this information to your [plastic pallet RFQ checklist](/resources/insights/2605-plastic-pallet-rfq-specification-checklist/) so suppliers can evaluate the real contact condition instead of quoting from weight alone.

## 2) Treat overhang as a handling risk, not just a space issue

Pallet overhang happens when cartons, bags, or containers extend beyond the pallet perimeter. It is common when teams try to maximize cube utilization, standardize one pallet size for many SKUs, or fit export container plans.

Small overhang may look harmless on the warehouse floor, but it creates several risks:

- outer cartons absorb forklift contact before the pallet does;
- stretch wrap tension pulls unsupported edges downward;
- stacked pallets transfer pressure through packaging instead of the pallet structure;
- rack entry becomes less forgiving because the visible load is wider than the pallet;
- transport vibration can loosen edge cartons and shift the unit load.

For routine handling, define an internal overhang limit by product type. Fragile cartons, bottled goods, bagged powders, and export loads with long transport legs usually need stricter control than durable molded parts.

A useful rule is to separate three conditions:

1. **No overhang required** — preferred for racking, automation, high-speed forklift traffic, and export loads with repeated transfers.
2. **Controlled minor overhang** — acceptable only after testing with actual packaging, wrap force, and handling route.
3. **Unacceptable overhang** — any condition where the product edge takes impact, the stack leans, labels become unreadable, or the load blocks safe fork entry.

The key is consistency. If one warehouse allows overhang and another rejects it, pallet damage data becomes unreliable and suppliers cannot diagnose root causes.

## 3) Match deck design to product behavior

Deck style should be selected according to how the load behaves under compression and movement.

### Open deck pallets

Open deck pallets are widely used because they are lighter, easier to clean, and suitable for many cartons, crates, and general warehouse loads. They work best when the product base has enough contact area to bridge deck openings without sagging.

They may require extra validation for:

- small cartons with narrow bottom panels,
- soft bags that deform into openings,
- thin corrugated packaging,
- products that must remain visually level for labeling or scanning.

### Closed deck pallets

Closed deck pallets provide more continuous surface support. They are often helpful for small packages, bagged goods, pails, hygiene-sensitive operations, or loads where product imprinting and bottom deformation must be reduced.

The tradeoff is that closed decks may add weight, affect cleaning/drying behavior, and change price. The decision should be based on product contact, sanitation requirements, and handling environment—not appearance alone.

### Runner and perimeter-base pallets

For racking, stacking, and heavy unit loads, bottom structure matters as much as the top deck. A stable base can reduce twisting, improve fork handling, and distribute forces more evenly through the pallet.

For example, a [1210 three-runner plastic pallet](/products/pallets/3-runner_1210/) can be considered when a buyer needs a common 1200 x 1000 mm footprint with a stable runner base for general warehouse circulation. The suitability still depends on the actual load footprint, rack span, and handling route.

## 4) Identify point-load risks before they become pallet defects

Point loads are concentrated forces applied to small deck areas. They often come from:

- metal bins with feet,
- equipment bases,
- crates with narrow runners,
- drums with raised bottom rings,
- stacked products where weight transfers through corner posts,
- mixed pallets where heavy SKUs sit on small contact zones.

A pallet may meet advertised load capacity under evenly distributed laboratory conditions but fail early under repeated point loading. This does not always mean the pallet was poorly made; it may mean the pallet was not specified for that contact pattern.

To control point-load risk, ask these questions before purchase:

- What is the smallest contact area touching the pallet deck?
- Does that contact area sit above a rib, runner, leg, or unsupported deck opening?
- Does the load remain centered during forklift acceleration and braking?
- Will the pallet be stacked, racked, or transported over long distances?
- Are there sharp edges, metal feet, or abrasive surfaces that can cut into the deck?

For high-risk loads, run a simple shop-floor test: place the real product on the pallet for the expected dwell time, lift it with the normal forklift, move it through the actual route, and inspect deck contact areas after repeated cycles. This does not replace formal load testing, but it reveals practical stress points that a catalog sheet may not show.

## 5) Build a unit-load approval checklist

A unit-load approval process prevents pallet decisions from being made by procurement alone. The best results usually come from a joint review by purchasing, warehouse operations, packaging engineering, quality, and safety.

Use the following checklist before bulk purchase:

1. **Product base map** — record carton size, bag dimensions, drum diameter, crate feet, or equipment support points.
2. **Palletizing pattern** — define layer count, column or interlock pattern, and whether mixed SKUs are allowed.
3. **Overhang rule** — set acceptable limits by product family and warehouse route.
4. **Contact alignment** — confirm that high-pressure zones sit on supported pallet areas.
5. **Stacking condition** — evaluate whether upper loads transfer force through product, packaging, or pallet edges.
6. **Handling route** — include forklift turns, dock plates, ramps, staging zones, rack entry, and truck loading.
7. **Packaging interaction** — test stretch wrap force, corner boards, slip sheets, and anti-slip materials.
8. **Inspection criteria** — define what counts as pallet damage, packaging damage, load shift, or unacceptable deflection.
9. **Approval sample** — retain one accepted pallet and one approved unit-load photo as the reference for future disputes.

This checklist is especially useful when multiple plants, suppliers, or 3PL warehouses handle the same SKU. It creates one shared definition of a good load instead of relying on local habits.

## 6) When a larger pallet is the better decision

Many teams try to solve overhang by tightening stacking rules. Sometimes that works. In other cases, the better decision is to change the pallet footprint.

A larger pallet may be justified when:

- the current pallet forces repeat overhang on high-volume SKUs;
- product damage costs exceed the pallet price difference;
- operators spend time reworking unstable loads;
- rack or truck loading plans allow the larger footprint;
- export customers reject loads with crushed edges or poor presentation;
- automation sensors or stretch wrappers require more consistent unit-load geometry.

The decision should be made on total operating cost. A smaller pallet that saves a few dollars per unit can become expensive if it increases packaging claims, forklift touches, manual rework, and shipment rejection.

Before switching size, confirm the downstream constraints: truck/container loading, rack beam dimensions, conveyor width, customer receiving rules, and return logistics. Pallet size is never only a warehouse choice; it affects the whole distribution chain.

---

## 7) How to write footprint requirements into a purchase specification

A clear specification reduces misunderstanding between buyer and supplier. Include language such as:

- pallet size and acceptable dimensional tolerance;
- product footprint drawing or photos;
- maximum permitted load overhang;
- minimum deck support needed for bags, cartons, drums, or crates;
- point-load locations and contact area;
- required static, dynamic, stacking, or racking condition;
- handling route and equipment type;
- inspection method after pilot use.

If the pallet will be used in racking, automation, freezer storage, or closed-loop return, add those operating conditions as separate requirements. Load footprint control should not replace broader validation; it should make that validation more realistic.

For a new pallet program, combine footprint review with a short field trial. A structured [90-day plastic pallet pilot](/resources/insights/2604-plastic-pallet-pilot-program/) gives the team enough time to observe repeated handling, product variation, and operator behavior before a full rollout.

---

## Final takeaway

Plastic pallet performance depends on more than material, weight rating, and price. It depends on how the product actually sits, moves, stacks, and transfers force through the pallet.

When buyers control overhang, identify point loads, match deck style to product behavior, and define unit-load approval rules before ordering, pallet selection becomes more predictable. The result is fewer damaged loads, fewer supplier disputes, and a warehouse operation that can scale without hidden handling problems.
